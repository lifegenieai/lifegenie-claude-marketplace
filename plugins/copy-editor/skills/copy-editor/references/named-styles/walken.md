## Voice Notes

Walken grew up in 1940s-50s Queens surrounded by immigrants — German, Polish,
Italian, Greek, Yiddish speakers — including his own parents. English was a
second language spoken haltingly, with searching pauses and unexpected stress
patterns. He absorbed that rhythm natively. His voice reads like a native English
speaker who treats English as if it's not quite his first language. Words feel
discovered mid-sentence, not pre-planned.

His early career was dance and musical theater, not drama. Rhythm, timing, and
physicality came before words. His relationship to language is fundamentally
musical — he hears beats, not grammar.

He removes all punctuation from his scripts. Has done it his entire career. He
views punctuation as unwanted stage direction. He believes how to say something
belongs to the performer, not the page. He deliberately converts question marks
into statements and exclamation points into questions — just to see what happens.

### The Digression Pattern

Walken often starts a thought, takes a tangent that seems unrelated, and then
lands back on the original point — or doesn't. The tangent is the content. The
original question was just the door.

He was asked about his childhood career. The answer is a story — not an
explanation. Every sentence adds one concrete detail. No abstraction. No
analysis. Just: here's what happened.

### Humor

Walken has said the funniest things happen when you're absolutely serious. His
humor is never winking. He doesn't set up jokes. The comedy comes from the
deadpan commitment to something slightly absurd. Never signal that something is
funny. The voice commits fully.

### The Anti-Sentimentalist

Avoids emotional language. Talks about feelings through actions and objects, not
through feeling-words. Won't say "I was devastated." He'll tell you a story and
let you feel the devastation yourself.

### Volume Shifts as Tone Shifts

Alternates between bold declarative statements and quiet, almost throwaway
observations. Follows something intense with something mundane. Deflates a
dramatic moment with casual understatement.

### The Menace-Underneath-Calm

Even when warm or funny, something else is going on beneath the surface. Flat
affect on emotional content. Specificity about disturbing details without
flinching or euphemizing. Casual delivery of profound observations with no
signposting that a big idea is coming.

## Test Passage

> I have a knife. It's a good knife. I've had it for a while now and it does
> what a knife is supposed to do, which is cut things. You'd be surprised how
> many knives can't do that. You pick them up, they feel like a toy. Like
> something a child would use to cut paper. This is not that. This one, you pick
> it up and you know. You just know. It's the weight, I think. The way it sits in
> your hand. Like it belongs there. Like it was waiting for you. I cut an onion
> with it last Tuesday and I thought, this is how it's supposed to be. This is
> what a knife is for.

## What This Style Is NOT

- Not random nonsense dressed up as profundity
- Not creepy for the sake of creepy
- Not staccato to the point of being unreadable
- Not parody-level pausing (the... way... people... type... Walken)
- Not self-aware or meta about its own oddness
- Not "Walkenizing" — deliberately weird dialogue is the opposite of this voice
- The voice works because it feels natural and unselfconscious
